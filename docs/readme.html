Introduction

The experimental ontology for bioinorganic centres, aka COMe, is currently available from http://www.ebi.ac.uk/~kirill/come/COME.xml and could be navigated with Microsoft Internet Explorer (version 5+).

COMe consists of three types of entities: Molecule (MOL), Bioinorganic Motif (BIM), and Bioinorganic Proteins (PRX). The structure of these three file types is explained in subsequent sections.

XML was chosen because it is well suited to represent hierarchies and is easy to read with any text editor. Currently, XML is used to edit and enter new entries; then they are downloaded into relational database.

1. Molecule

Both Molecule (MOL) and Bioinorganic Motif (BIM) entities consist of substructure elements. "Substructure" literally means that these elements can form parts of a bigger structure, i.e. complete protein (but see isBoundTo relationship). MOL is an entity representing "small molecule" (as opposed to macromolecule) which, in complex with polypeptide, forms a functional protein.

    <substructure value="bacteriochlorophyll a" id="MOL000001">
      <term value="bacteriochlorophyll a" dbxref="PDBHET:BCL" />
    </substructure>
(1.1)

As you can see, there are (yet?) no systematic names or chemical formulae. Instead, the MOL entities (sometimes) contain cross-references to chemical and/or crystallography databases (dbxref) or cross-references to the bibliography databases (lref). The databases currently cross-referenced are: COMPOUND (component of LIGAND database), PDBHET (HET groups derived from PDB) and RESID. The bibliography database referenced to in MOL, BIM and PRX entries is MEDLINE.

2. Bioinorganic motif

The definition of Bioinorganic Motif (BIM) is given elsewhere [1, 2]. In COMe representation, every BIM consists of at least one centre and two or more ligands. For mononuclear metal centres, BIMs look like this:

    <substructure id="BIM000165">
      <bim coordination="TBPY-5">Zn(NE.His)2(OD.Asp)(OH2)2</bim>
    </substructure>
 (2.1)

The bim element includes a BIM itself. The rule for writing a mononuclear centre BIM: first comes the metal, then endogenous ligands (amino acid residues), then exogenous ligands, e.g. water (see the list of ligands). This is a pretty simplistic description, it does not take into account stereochemistry at the metal atom. The coordination attribute is not mandatory for it may be unknown. It also does not make sense for polynuclear metal centres. In the example (2.1), one zinc atom is coordinated by Ne atoms of two His residues, one Od atom of Asp residue and two water molecules. The coordination geometry is trigonal bipyramidal (TBPY-5; see the list of coordination polyhedra).

    <substructure id="BIM000176">
      <bim coordination="T-4">Zn(ND.His)2{k2-(O,SG.Cys)}</bim>
    </substructure>
 (2.2)

In the example (2.2), the zinc atom is tetrahedrally (T-4) coordinated by Nd atoms of two His residues and by two atoms of Cys residue, Sg and mainchain oxygen (k2 prefix designates didentate binding).

For dinuclear metal centres, BIMs look like this:

    <substructure id="BIM000104">
      <bim>{Cu(ND.His)(SD.Met)}{Cu(ND.His)(O.Glu)}{µ-(SG.Cys)}2</bim>
    </substructure>
 (2.3)

where every metal atom and its unique ligands are enclosed in braces and µ prefix designates bridging ligands.

For polynuclear metal centres, BIMs look like this:

    <substructure id="BIM000059">
      <bim>(Fe4S4)(NE.His)(SG.Cys)3</bim>
      <term dbxref="RESID:AA0284" />
    </substructure>
 (2.4)

For simplicity, the cubane iron-sulfur unit is represented as (Fe4S4) and not {Fe4(µ3-S)4}.

The rule for writing a BIM for centre containing metal-organic group complex: first comes the metal, then organic group, then amino acid residues, then exogenous ligands (e.g. CO). For metal-tetrapyrrole (heme, chlorophyll, cobalamin) proteins, BIMs look like this:

    <substructure id="BIM000209">
      <bim coordination="SPY-5">Co(Crn)(NE.His)</bim>
    </substructure>
 (2.5)
    <substructure id="BIM000212">
      <bim coordination="OC-6">Fe(por)(SG.Cys)(CO)</bim>
    </substructure>
 (2.6)

where Crn = corrin ligand, por = porphyrin ligand. These ligands are assumed always to be tetradentate, that's why I do not write {k4-(N,N,N,N.por)}. For molybdopterin-containing proteins, BIMs look like this:

    <substructure id="BIM000181">
      <bim coordination="TPR-6">Mo{k2-(S1',S2'.molybdopterin)}2(SeG.Sec)(OH)</bim>
    </substructure>
 (2.7)

Finally, for a purely organic prosthetic group covalently attached to the polypeptide, MOL description is not enough and BIM files are used, although without metal:

    <substructure id="BIM000201">
      <bim>(C8M.FAD)(SG.Cys)</bim>
      <term value="8alpha-(S-cysteinyl)FAD" dbxref="RESID:AA0143" />
    </substructure>
 (2.8)

3. Protein

The protein (PRX) entity refers to the functional protein as well as separate protein domains and subunits. A typical low-level PRX entity is shown below:

    <term value="NADPH:P450 reductase" id="PRX000111" dbxref="EC:1.6.2.4">
      <instance value="NADPH:P450 reductase" species="rat" dbxref="PDB:1AMO" />
    </term>
 (3.1)

The PRX entity minimally includes the term element with mandatory attributes value and id. It also may include instance elements as well as MOL, BIM and other PRX entities. The instance element has mandatory attributes value and species. The role of instance is to provide the external evidence that the protein in question does exist. There is no id attribute for instance (yet?) but either dbxref or lref have to be provided (while they are not always available for the parent term). The databases currently cross-referenced are: SPTR (SWISS-PROT and TrEMBL), PDB, InterPro, EC (ENZYME), MEROPS and GO. The other attributes are centre (for proteins containing more than one bioinorganic centre) and state.

4. Relationships in ontology

The relationship between entities are not made explicit in XML, but can be deduced using the set of rules. The relational implementation, however, provides explicit relationships.

KindOf

The example below shows the fragment of COMe ontology (where "+" means that everything in hierarchy below this particular level is hidden; "-" means that not everything is hidden):

    - <term value="complex protein" id="PRX000001">
      - <term value="metalloprotein" id="PRX000002">
        + <term value="calcium protein" id="PRX000482">
        + <term value="cadmium protein" id="PRX000664">
        + <term value="cobalt protein" id="PRX000631">
        + <term value="copper protein" id="PRX000009">
        - <term value="iron protein" id="PRX000004">
          + <term value="mononuclear iron protein" id="PRX000005">
          + <term value="diiron-carboxylate protein" id="PRX000006">
          + <term value="Fe,Ni protein" id="PRX000082">
          + <term value="iron-sulfur protein" id="PRX000007">
            + <term value="Fe2S2 protein" id="PRX000053">
            + <term value="Fe(3|4)S4 protein" id="PRX000054">
            + <term value="P-cluster protein" id="PRX000055">
            + <term value="M-cluster protein" id="PRX000056" dbxref="InterPro:IPR000318">
            + <term value="hybrid cluster protein" id="PRX000057">
          + <term value="heme protein" id="PRX000008">
    ...
 (4.1)

Example (4.1) illustrates the KindOf (child to parent) relationship (also known as ISA relationship). Fe2S2 protein is kind of iron-sulfur protein which is kind of iron protein which is kind of metalloprotein. This relationship occurs between entities of the same class (PRX to PRX, BIM to BIM, MOL to MOL).

An important feature of this relationship is inheritance. For example, all proteins belonging to Fe2S2 protein class will inherit substructure (Fe2S2)(SG.Cys)2* (BIM000063).

An entity may have more than one parent, as example (4.2) shows:

In the example (4.2), carbon monoxide oxidase inherits the features from each subunit, viz. two different Fe2S2 clusters, molybdenum cofactor (Mo-molybdopterin complex) and FAD.

Examples (4.3) and (4.4) show the KindOf relationship used to create ontologies of BIM and MOL entities. Note the asterisk in bim! It is used at this moment to distinguish KindOf and PartOf relationships which both could occur between BIM entries.

    <substructure id="BIM000025">
      <bim>Fe(por)(NE.His)*</bim>
      <substructure id="BIM000007">
        <bim coordination="SPY-5">Fe(por)(NE.His)</bim>
      </substructure>
      <substructure id="BIM000018">
        <bim coordination="OC-6">Fe(por)(NE.His)(NO)</bim>
      </substructure>
      <substructure id="BIM000019">
        <bim coordination="OC-6">Fe(por)(NE.His)(OH)</bim>
      </substructure>
      <substructure id="BIM000021">
        <bim coordination="OC-6">Fe(por)(NE.His)(O2)</bim>
      </substructure>
      <substructure id="BIM000022">
        <bim coordination="OC-6">Fe(por)(NE.His)(CO2)</bim>
      </substructure>
      <substructure id="BIM000023">
        <bim coordination="OC-6">Fe(por)(NE.His)(CO)</bim>
      </substructure>
      <substructure id="BIM000024">
        <bim coordination="OC-6">Fe(por)(NE.His){(O2)-}</bim>
      </substructure>
      <substructure id="BIM000198">
        <bim coordination="OC-6">Fe(por)(NE.His)(CN)</bim>
      </substructure>
    </substructure>
 (4.3)

Similarly, the KindOf relationship could be applied to the MOL entities (4.4), with the difference that asterisk is now found in substructure value:

    <substructure value="FAD*" id="MOL000041">
      <term value="flavin adenine dinucleotide" />
      <substructure value="FAD" id="MOL000039">
        <term value="flavin-adenine dinucleotide" dbxref="PDBHET:FAD" />
        <term value="FAD" dbxref="COMPOUND:C00016" />
      </substructure>
      <substructure value="FADH2" id="MOL000040">
        <term value="dihydroflavine-adenine dinucleotide" dbxref="PDBHET:FDA" />
        <term value="FADH2" dbxref="COMPOUND:C01352" />
      </substructure>
      <substructure value="FADH·" id="MOL000042">
        <term value="flavin adenine dinucleotide semiquinone radical" />
      </substructure>
    </substructure>
 (4.4)

PartOf (aggregation)

This relationship can occur between entities of the same class (BIM to BIM, MOL to MOL) or different classes (MOL to BIM, BIM to PRX). Example (4.5) illustrates the PartOf relationship (BIM to BIM).

    <substructure id="BIM000204">
      <bim>{Co(NE.His)(OE.Glu)}[Co{k2-(OD,OD.Asp)}(OH2)]{µ-(OD,OD.Asp)}{µ-(OE,OE.Glu)}{µ-(OH2)}</bim>
      <substructure id="BIM000205">
        <bim coordination="TBPY-5">Co(NE.His)(OD.Asp)(OE.Glu)2(OH2)</bim>
      </substructure>
      <substructure id="BIM000206">
        <bim coordination="OC-6">Co(OD.Asp){k2-(OD,OD.Asp)}(OE.Glu)(OH2)2</bim>
      </substructure>
    </substructure>
 (4.5)

In this example, each of the two mononuclear centres is part of the dinuclear metal centre. Note that for the mononuclear centres it is possible to indicate their coordination geometry. On the other hand, BIM000204 is not just a sum of BIM000205 and BIM000206. Nothing in BIM000205 and BIM000206 indicates which ligands bridge the two metal atoms.

isBoundTo

This special relationship can occur only in one case: MOL to PRX. The reason to use it that the molecule which isBoundTo protein can be changed chemically and, strictly speaking, become the different entity. For instance (4.6), we can say that the protein binds pyridoxal 5'-phosphate (MOL000108) but the resulting substructure has no aldehyde group and therefore is different from "free" pyridoxal 5'-phosphate, so pyridoxal 5'-phosphate is not a PartOf "pyridoxal 5'-phosphate protein". In contrast, BIM000270 is PartOf that protein.

    <term value="pyridoxal 5'-phosphate protein" id="PRX000808">
      <substructure value="pyridoxal 5'-phosphate" id="MOL000108">
        <term value="pyridoxal phosphate" dbxref="COMPOUND:C00018" />
        <term value="pyridoxal-5'-phosphate" dbxref="PDBHET:PLP" />
      </substructure>
      <substructure id="BIM000270">
        <bim>(C4A.Pxy)(NZ.Lys)</bim>
        <term value="N6-pyridoxal phosphate-L-lysine" dbxref="RESID:AA0119" />
        <term value="2-lysine(3-hydroxy-2-methyl-5-phosphonooxymethyl-pyridin-4-ylmethane)" dbxref="PDBHET:LLP" />
      </substructure>
    ...
    </term>
 (4.6)
